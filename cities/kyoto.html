Kyoto · 京都

Kyoto

4 nights · the cultural anchor of the trip
京都

Kyoto is the deliberate counterpoint to Tokyo — temple gardens, geisha districts, tea houses, wooden machiya alleys. It's also relentlessly crowded at the famous sights, so the trick is going early or late. The center is walkable, the buses are tolerable, and a half-day private guide on arrival makes everything else easier.

Where we stayed

  • Stay

    The Royal Park Hotel Kyoto Sanjo

    Central, steps from Pontocho and the Kamogawa river, walking distance to Gion. A well-located, well-priced business hotel — not a ryokan, but you have those in Hakone and Koyasan.

The famous sights, in order

  • See

    Fushimi Inari Taisha

    The thousand vermillion torii gates winding up the mountain. Free, open 24/7. Go before 8am or after sunset to skip the crowds — the lower gates are unwalkable by mid-morning. Hike at least to the Yotsutsuji intersection for the view; full loop is about 2 hours.

  • See

    Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion)

    The gold-leaf temple pavilion floating in a pond. A surprisingly quick visit — 30 minutes is plenty. Pair it with Ryoan-ji next door.

  • See

    Ryoan-ji

    The famous Zen rock garden. Take ten quiet minutes on the wooden veranda. The temple grounds and walking path around the pond are lovely.

  • See

    Kiyomizu-dera

    Massive wooden temple on stilts above the city. The approach — up Ninenzaka and Sannenzaka, lined with tea shops and machiya houses — is half the experience. Don't skip the Otowa waterfall inside.

  • See

    Arashiyama Bamboo Grove

    Western edge of Kyoto. The grove itself is small — under 500m — and packs out fast. Sunrise is the only way to have it to yourself.

  • See

    Arashiyama Monkey Park Iwatayama

    30-minute uphill hike from the base of Arashiyama, ending in a hilltop clearing full of wild macaques and a panoramic view of Kyoto. Combine it with the bamboo grove the same morning.

  • See

    Nishiki Market

    Five-block covered food street downtown. Pickles, sweets, sashimi sticks, tako tamago, Aritsugu knives. Best as a grazing lunch.

Neighborhoods at night

  • Neighborhood

    Pontocho Alley

    Narrow lantern-lit cobbled lane along the Kamogawa, lined with restaurants from cheap yakitori to high-end kaiseki. Walk it at dusk; if you're there May–September, book somewhere with a yuka riverside terrace.

  • Neighborhood

    Gion

    The old geisha district, just across the river. Wooden machiya houses, lantern light, tea houses. Be a respectful tourist — no chasing geiko down alleys for photos. The main Hanamikoji street has had photography restrictions since 2019.

Where we ate

  • Eat · $$$

    Kikunoi Roan

    The Pontocho outpost of the Michelin-starred Kikunoi — contemporary kaiseki in a more intimate, accessible format than the main Higashiyama house. Reserve well in advance.

  • Eat · $$$

    Nineizaka Sushi Numamoto (二寧坂 鮨沼本)

    Quiet, intimate sushi counter on Ninenzaka in Higashiyama. Reserve.

  • Eat · $$

    Yamafuku (shabu-shabu)

    Pontocho-area shabu-shabu specialist — thin-sliced wagyu cooked in dashi at the table.

  • Eat · $$

    Yaruki Yakiniku

    Casual yakiniku — grill-your-own Japanese BBQ — in central Kyoto. Good end to a long temple day.

  • Drink

    L'Escamoteur Bar

    Dim, theatrical apothecary-style cocktail bar just off Kawaramachi-dori. Excellent nightcap if you can squeeze in at the counter.

Things to book ahead

  • Do

    Private Kyoto day tour

    A 6-hour private guided day on arrival (we used ToursByLocals) covered Kinkaku-ji, Ryoan-ji, and Kiyomizu-dera in one efficient sweep. Worth it day one — everything else self-guided after.

  • Do

    Maiko evening performance

    A short chaperoned dinner show with a maiko performing classical dance — the most beginner-friendly way to actually see one perform live. We booked through Veltra; Yasakadori Enraku in Gion was the host venue.
