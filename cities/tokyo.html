Where we stayed

  • Stay

    Hotel Gracery Shinjuku

    Big chain hotel in Kabukicho with a Godzilla head on the roof. Convenient first base — walking distance to Shinjuku Station, eight different train lines, and everything open late.

  • Stay

    Keio Plaza Hotel Tokyo

    Used for the final stretch. West Shinjuku, quieter than Kabukicho, near the limousine bus to Narita — useful if you have an early flight out.

Neighborhoods to walk

  • Neighborhood

    Shimo-Kitazawa

    Indie, low-rise neighborhood west of Shibuya. Thrift shops, vintage record stores, tiny cafes, live music venues. The Tokyo most travelers miss. Easy half-day on the Keio Inokashira or Odakyu line.

  • Neighborhood

    Harajuku & Omotesando

    Pair Takeshita Street's teen-pop chaos with the tree-lined Omotesando avenue for high-low contrast. Best combined with Meiji Shrine in one loop.

  • Neighborhood

    Ginza

    Tokyo's polished shopping district. The main streets close to cars on weekend afternoons — pleasant for window-shopping and people-watching.

Sights worth your time

  • See

    Shibuya Crossing

    Yes, do it. The view is much better from above — the Starbucks at Tsutaya is free and packed, the new Shibuya Sky observation deck is paid and worth it.

  • See

    Meiji Shrine

    Forested Shinto shrine tucked behind Harajuku. Gravel paths, towering torii gates, eerily quiet given where it is. Go in the morning.

  • See

    Imperial Palace Grounds

    The inner palace only opens twice a year, but the outer grounds and East Gardens are free and easy to combine with Tokyo Station. Bring a hat in summer.

  • See

    Tsukiji Outer Market

    The famous inner fish auction moved to Toyosu in 2018, but the outer market is still the best breakfast in the city — tamago skewers, uni shooters, fresh sashimi rice bowls. Go hungry, go early.

  • See

    Tokyo City View & Sky Deck

    Roppongi Hills observatory. The Sky Deck on top is the only open-air rooftop observation in Tokyo — surprisingly few people know about it.

  • See

    teamLab (Digital Art Museum)

    Immersive room-scale digital art. The original Borderless venue in Odaiba relocated to Azabudai Hills in 2024 — the experience is the same in spirit. Book a timed entry well in advance. Plan two to three hours inside.

  • See

    Odaiba

    Reclaimed island in Tokyo Bay. Rainbow Bridge views, oversized shopping malls, Gundam statue, the spa-and-yukata complex Oedo-Onsen (note: the original Oedo-Onsen closed in 2021; check what's currently open).

  • See

    Tokyo DisneySea

    If you only ever do one Disney park, do this one. It exists nowhere else in the world and the theming is genuinely incredible. Full day, weekday if possible. Buy tickets online before you fly.

Where we ate

  • Eat · $$$

    Sushi Bar Yasuda

    Quiet 8-seat omakase counter in Minato run by Naomichi Yasuda — the chef who made his name at Sushi Yasuda in New York. Reserve weeks ahead.

  • Eat · $$

    Shinjuku Warayakiya

    Straw-fired bonito tataki (katsuo no tataki) cooked tableside. Loud, casual, great fun in a group.

  • Eat · $

    Nakaya (Tsukiji)

    Standing-counter spot in the Tsukiji outer market — good for a fresh chirashi breakfast.

  • Eat · $

    Totti Candy Factory

    Rainbow cotton candy bigger than your head on Takeshita Street, Harajuku. Touristy and absurd; you'll still take the photo.

  • Eat · $

    mois cafe

    Tiny corner cafe in Shimo-Kitazawa — useful pit stop while wandering.

Nightlife & oddities

  • Do

    Robot Restaurant (now: Samurai Restaurant)

    The legendary Kabukicho neon-and-robots dinner show. The original closed during the pandemic — a "Samurai Restaurant" successor has opened in roughly the same venue and vein. Tacky, loud, fun in a group of four.
