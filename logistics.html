Practical stuff

Trains, SIMs, budget.

The handful of things worth booking before you fly — and roughly what it all cost.

Getting around

JR Pass (Japan Rail Pass)

The single most useful thing for a trip that spans Tokyo–Kyoto–Osaka. Covers nearly every shinkansen (except the fastest Nozomi and Mizuho) plus most JR local lines within cities. Sold in 7-, 14-, and 21-day versions.

Heads-up: in October 2023 the JR Pass had a major price hike — a 14-day pass roughly doubled. Run the numbers against individual shinkansen tickets first; the math now only works if you're doing several long-distance legs. For a Tokyo→Kyoto→ Osaka→Tokyo loop it's still typically a wash or a small win.

Buy before you fly through an authorized reseller (JRPass.com, JTB, etc.). They ship you an exchange voucher; you trade it for the actual pass at a JR ticket office on arrival.

Suica or Pasmo (IC card)

The tap-to-pay card for subways, buses, convenience stores, and vending machines all over Japan. Get one at the airport on arrival or load Suica into Apple Wallet before you leave. Top up at any station.

Local-only passes worth knowing

Hakone Free Pass (Odakyu) — round-trip Shinjuku → Hakone + the loop of mountain railway, ropeway, pirate boat, and local buses.

Koyasan-Sekai-Isan Kippu (Nankai) — round-trip Namba → Koyasan including the cable car and mountain buses. JR Pass does not cover this leg.

Connectivity

SIM card or eSIM

In 2018 we ordered a Sakura Mobile 5GB physical SIM each for ~$55 and picked it up at the Narita arrivals counter. Worked everywhere, including Koyasan.

These days an eSIM from Airalo, Ubigi, or Saily is faster and cheaper — install before you fly, activate on landing. 5GB for 15 days runs $15–25.

Pocket WiFi

Still useful if four+ devices need to share — Ninja WiFi and Sakura Mobile both do airport pickup. eSIM has mostly made these obsolete for couples.

Roughly what it cost

The 2018 trip ran ~$6,400 for two people over 16 days, or ~$3,180 per person before in-the-moment food and shopping. Inflation, the weak yen, and the post-2023 JR Pass hike make today's costs different in both directions — flights are similar, hotels are pricier, but the yen makes restaurant meals notably cheaper than 2018.

A few practical notes

Cash is still a thing

Most restaurants, especially in Kyoto and on Koyasan, take cash only or have a card minimum. Pull yen at any 7-Eleven ATM or post office — both reliably take foreign cards.

Luggage forwarding (takkyubin)

You can ship a suitcase city-to-city through services like Yamato Transport from any convenience store or hotel concierge for ~¥2,000. Hugely useful before Koyasan or Hakone, where you don't want to drag bags up the mountain.

Show up to dinner reservations on time

Almost every restaurant we booked had a 15-minute grace window before automatic cancellation. Cancellation fees can run the full price of the meal for omakase. Use Google Maps to time door-to-door.
